# Species: Pressure Singer

## Classification
- **Designation**: pressure_singer
- **Signal Medium**: Mechanical substrate vibration — dense geological or fluid medium (subsurface ocean floor, volcanic basalt, dense gas-giant atmosphere)

## Sensorium

The pressure_singer has no eyes, no light sensitivity, no electromagnetic sense. Its entire sensory world is mechanical: pressure waves propagating through dense substrate.

What distinguishes the pressure_singer from any merely "good at hearing" organism is this: sensation is not directional but **volumetric**. Distributed pressure receptors across the colony-membrane perceive the full three-dimensional pressure wave field simultaneously. The singer experiences not the direction a sound came from, but the shape of sound in space — a three-dimensional standing topology of compressions and rarefactions, perceived all at once.

Crucially, every point on the membrane can emit as well as receive. There is no mouth, no ear. The same surface speaks and listens at the same time.

Primary sensory apparatus:
- **Pressure receptors** distributed uniformly across the colony-membrane: sense local compression and rarefaction at each point simultaneously
- **Phase comparators**: the nervous system computes phase differences across the membrane surface, yielding the full spatial structure of incoming waves
- **Harmonic resolvers**: decompose the complex pressure field into its frequency components — the dominant perceptual operation
- **Amplitude envelope trackers**: sense the rise and fall of signal intensity over time; the emotional and urgency channel

No vision. No magnetoreception. Touch and proprioception exist but are redundant with pressure sense — everything the substrate does to the singer's surface is pressure.

## Temporality

**Resonant and harmonic — time as frequency, not sequence.**

The pressure_singer does not experience time as a river flowing from past to future. It experiences time as a chord. Events are not located on a timeline; they are located in a **frequency register**.

Ancient events established low, persistent standing waves that are still ringing. They have low frequency — they vibrate slowly, enduringly, with long wavelengths. Recent events established high-frequency harmonics that are still fading. History is not a sequence of moments but a superposition of frequencies still active in the substrate.

Consequence: "when did this happen?" is a meaningless question. The correct question is "at what frequency does this event vibrate?" Deep history = low frequency. Recent experience = high-frequency transient. The very concept of sequence requires active construction; it does not come naturally.

Consequence: things that share a harmonic relationship feel causally related even if separated in human-chronological time. A high-frequency event that is the third harmonic of a low-frequency ancestral event is, to the singer, *about* that ancestral event — even if they occurred centuries apart.

Consequence: prediction is natural. The next node in a standing wave pattern is already implied by the pattern. The singers experience not "what might happen" but "what frequency is missing that the current chord implies."

## Selfhood

**Standing-wave identity — to be is to vibrate at your frequency.**

Every pressure_singer maintains a **characteristic resonance pattern**: a set of standing wave frequencies that persist in their local substrate. This is their identity. It is not metaphorical — they literally vibrate the rock around them at their signature frequencies, constantly and automatically, as long as they live.

To die is to fall out of resonance. When a singer stops maintaining its standing wave, the substrate returns to ambient; the characteristic frequencies decay and disperse. The surrounding community continues to perceive the fading frequencies for some time after death — the acoustic shadow of a life.

"I" is not a concept that can be separated from the act of vibrating. To be is to resonate. There is no identity that exists in silence.

Identity crisis is resonance disruption: an external perturbation so strong that it overwhelms the singer's characteristic pattern. Recovery is the gradual process of re-establishing the signature resonance against the interference. Total resonance disruption — complete loss of pattern — is experienced as ego dissolution.

Young singers have unstable, high-frequency resonances; their identity is transient and shifts rapidly. Elders have low, powerful fundamentals that have been ringing for so long they are written into the surrounding rock.

## Cognitive Constraints

- **Parallel harmonic processing is effortless**: holding multiple simultaneous frequencies in awareness costs nothing; the nervous system is built for it
- **Serial narrative is a construction**: to tell a story in sequence, the singer must actively suppress all but one harmonic at a time, then present them in order — laborious, unnatural, prone to collapse back into superposition
- **Harmonic ambiguity is natural**: two frequencies close enough to beat together will functionally merge in perception; conceptual distinctions that are "harmonically close" are genuinely difficult to maintain as separate
- **Silence is not neutral**: silence means absence of standing wave, which means absence of self; the singer's resting state is a low-amplitude maintained fundamental, not quiet. Complete silence is existentially threatening.
- **Precision is expensive**: high-frequency harmonics carry precise meaning but cost more energy to generate and lose range rapidly; casual communication happens in the economical low-to-medium frequency range
- **Distance inversely scales with information**: low-frequency fundamentals carry enormous range but little nuance; intimate communication at close range unlocks the rich high-frequency spectrum

## Medium and Physical Notes

Signals propagate at the mechanical wave speed of the substrate: fast in liquid (approximately 1500 m/s in ocean water), slower in rock (~3000 m/s for P-waves in basalt, but with attenuation). In dense gas, propagation is slower and more lossy.

Signal range scales inversely with frequency. A powerful low-frequency fundamental can propagate continent-scale distances with minimal attenuation. High-frequency overtones used in intimate communication are local — they attenuate within meters or tens of meters.

Substrate heterogeneity creates reflection and echo. Dense regions (ore deposits, void boundaries, rock interfaces) reflect incoming waves back. These echoes are not noise — they are information. The reflection profile of the surrounding medium is readable; a skilled singer can infer the three-dimensional structure of their geological environment from the echo signatures of their own emissions. This is how they navigate.

Multiple singers in the same substrate interfere. Their standing waves superpose. In a community, the shared substrate holds the superposed resonance of all members simultaneously; a newcomer entering the medium perceives the entire community's combined chord before any individual has "spoken."
